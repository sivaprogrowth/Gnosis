## Abstract

A 9-section playbook from Emergence Capital (Sarah Guo / Gordon Ritter-era enterprise VC, now investing in the category) for founders and operators building AI-native services (AINS) companies. Framed as the first attempt at a playbook for a business model that didn't exist before 2023. The playbook argues AINS is a "defining business model of the AI era" because it collapses software + services into one integrated system, owns the full outcome, and can hit 50%+ gross margins at 5-10× the throughput of legacy services. Source for [[ai-native-services]], [[mirage-pmf]], [[north-star-product-metric]], and [[outcome-based-pricing]].

## Key claims

- **AINS is a distinct business model, not a SaaS variant.** The customer buys an outcome delivered by the AINS provider, not a product they implement themselves. This collapses SaaS and services into one integrated system.
- **Mirage PMF is the category's most dangerous failure mode.** Revenue growth and logo retention can mask a lack of real AI leverage. Real PMF requires "scaling non-linearly relative to cost" — if gross margin stays flat as revenue grows, the company is a traditional services firm with venture capital underneath.
- **Domain expertise is existential, not a nice-to-have.** Buyers evaluate AINS providers on reputation and credibility of the people delivering, because there's no product demo to anchor trust. Every case study in the playbook (Mechanical Orchard, Harper, Hanover Park, Crosby Legal) hired or was founded by someone with deep category credibility.
- **Downmarket often productizes faster than enterprise.** Lower ACVs force productization (you can't throw human labor at each engagement), homogeneity reduces edge cases, simpler workflows demonstrate AI leverage faster. Harper's Main Street focus (daycares, manufacturers, restaurants) is the canonical example.
- **Delivery IS the product, not a support function.** AINS companies should staff dedicated pilot teams ("SEAL teams"), rotate people between delivery and product so learnings flow back, sit domain experts next to engineers for real-time feedback, and start every board meeting with customer health.
- **Outcome-based pricing is the natural pricing model for AINS.** Because the service IS the outcome, attribution is solved by definition. Two implementations: **project-based** for discrete work (mainframe migration, complex claim), **credits-based** for continuous variable work (customer service streams, claim volume).
- **Every AINS company needs a North Star Product Metric** — a single number that captures how much of the work AI is actually doing. Crosby Legal uses "HURT" (Human Review Time in minutes per document). As the metric approaches zero, margins approach software margins. Revenue per Employee is the universal lagging indicator.
- **Data flywheel from day one** is the primary moat. MSAs must grant data-use rights. The data generated *by doing the work* IS the compounding advantage — a services company that uses AI tools is not AINS; an AINS gets qualitatively better every engagement.

## Why this matters for [[progrowth]]

ProGrowth is structurally an AINS company — fractional CMO + AI marketing + automation + AI video. The playbook gives it a vocabulary and a diagnostic framework:

- Is ProGrowth hitting [[mirage-pmf]]? Needs a [[north-star-product-metric]] and honest gross-margin accounting.
- ProGrowth's ICP decision (mid-market B2B — credit unions, insurance, professional services) matches the playbook's "downmarket productizes faster" thesis.
- ProGrowth's [[ai-overview-tool]] is a product layer on top of the services layer — the Palantir pattern referenced in §VI Pricing (custom analyses + leave-behind software for recurring revenue).
- Marketri is a non-AI-native services firm: the vulnerability framework in [[mirage-pmf]] and this playbook's AI-leverage metrics point to where they're structurally weak.
